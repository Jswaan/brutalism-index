Brutalist Architecture

Brutalist architecture emerged in the mid-20th century, standing out for its straightforward, unembellished appearance, mainly showcasing the extensive use of concrete. This architectural style is characterized by its preference for raw, unrefined surfaces, where the texture and imperfections of concrete are not only accepted but celebrated. Buildings designed in this style often adopt geometric, repetitive shapes, creating stark, monumental forms that can appear imposing or even fortress-like. In Brutalism, the functional aspects of a structure are emphasized, with components such as staircases, elevators, and service areas often exposed, adding to the building's visual narrative. Despite their hefty appearance, Brutalist buildings are thoughtfully integrated with their surroundings, utilizing natural light and incorporating green spaces, thereby attempting to harmonize with their environment rather than dominate it. The Brutalist movement was also imbued with social utopian ideals, especially evident in its application to public buildings, educational institutions, and housing projects. The aim was to foster community and equality through architecture, though the realization of these ideals was met with varying degrees of success. Over the years, Brutalist architecture has elicited mixed reactions, with some viewing it as cold and unwelcoming due to its heavy use of concrete and its massive scale. Others, however, have come to appreciate the raw beauty and honesty of Brutalist buildings, recognizing them as expressive works of art that truthfully reflect their materials and purpose. Notable examples of Brutalist architecture, such as the Barbican Estate in London, Habitat 67 in Montreal, Boston City Hall, and the National Assembly Building in Dhaka, Bangladesh, showcase the style's diversity and its potential to create functional, yet visually striking spaces. These buildings, among others, exemplify the Brutalist ethos of creating integrated environments that serve both practical and aesthetic purposes. Recently, there has been a resurgence of interest in Brutalist architecture, with a growing appreciation for its bold aesthetic and architectural significance. This renewed interest has sparked efforts to preserve and repurpose iconic Brutalist structures, recognizing them as valuable cultural and historical artifacts that continue to influence contemporary architectural thought. Despite its controversial nature, Brutalist architecture remains a fascinating chapter in the history of design, challenging perceptions of beauty and functionality in the built environment.

브루탈리즘 건축

20세기 중반에 등장한 브루탈리즘 건축물은 단순하고 꾸밈이 없는 외관으로 눈에 띄며 주로 콘크리트를 광범위하게 사용했다. 이 건축 스타일은 콘크리트의 질감과 불완전성을 수용할 뿐만 아니라 기념하는 원시적이고 정제되지 않은 표면을 선호하는 것이 특징이다. 이 스타일로 설계된 건물은 종종 기하학적이고 반복적인 모양을 채택하여 인상적이거나 심지어 요새처럼 보일 수 있는 뚜렷하고 기념비적인 형태를 만든다. 브루탈리즘(Brutalism)에서는 계단, 엘리베이터, 서비스 구역 등의 구성요소가 노출되는 경우가 많아 건물의 기능적 측면이 강조되어 건물의 시각적 서사를 더해줍니다. 육중한 외관에도 불구하고, 브루탈리즘 건물은 자연광을 활용하고 녹지 공간을 통합하여 주변 환경과 세심하게 통합되어 환경을 지배하기보다는 환경과 조화를 이루려고 시도한다. 브루탈리스트 운동은 또한 사회적 유토피아적 이상으로 가득 차 있었는데, 특히 공공 건물, 교육 기관 및 주택 프로젝트에 적용할 때 더욱 분명해졌다. 목표는 건축을 통해 공동체와 평등을 육성하는 것이었고 이러한 이상의 실현은 다양한 수준의 성공을 거두었다. 수년에 걸쳐 브루탈리즘 건축은 엇갈린 반응을 불러일으켰고, 일부에서는 콘크리트를 많이 사용하고 대규모 규모로 인해 차갑고 환영받지 못한다고 생각했다.

그러나 다른 사람들은 브루탈리즘 건물의 원시적인 아름다움과 정직함을 높이 평가하고 그 재료와 목적을 진실하게 반영하는 표현적인 예술 작품으로 인식하게 되었다. 런던의 Barbican Estate, 몬트리올의 Habitat 67, 보스턴 시청, 방글라데시 다카의 국회 의사당과 같은 주목할만한 브루탈리즘 건축의 사례는 스타일의 다양성과 기능적이면서도 시각적으로 눈에 띄는 공간을 만들 수 있는 잠재력을 보여준다. 무엇보다도 이러한 건물은 실용적이고 미학적 목적을 모두 충족하는 통합 환경을 만드는 브루탈리즘 정신을 예시한다. 최근에는 대담한 미적, 건축학적 중요성에 대한 인식이 높아지면서 브루탈리즘 건축에 대한 관심이 다시 높아지고 있습니다. 이러한 새로운 관심은 상징적인 브루탈리즘 건축물을 보존하고 용도를 변경하려는 노력을 촉발시켰으며, 이를 현대 건축 사상에 계속 영향을 미치는 귀중한 문화 및 역사적 유물로 인식했다. 논란의 여지가 있는 성격에도 불구하고 브루탈리즘 건축은 건축 환경의 아름다움과 기능성에 대한 인식에 도전하면서 디자인 역사에서 매혹적인 장으로 남아 있다.

Geisel Library San Diego, USA

32.88116°N 117.237651°W

Geisel Library is the main library building of the University of California, San Diego. It is named in honor of Audrey and Theodor Seuss Geisel, better known as children's author Dr. Seuss. The building's distinctive architecture, described as occupying "a fascinating nexus between brutalism and futurism", has resulted in its being featured in the UC San Diego logo and becoming the most recogni zable building on campus. The library was designed by William Pereira and opened in 1970 as the Central Library. It was renovated in 1993 and rededicated as the University Library Building, and renamed Geisel Library in 1995.[2] The UC San Diego Library consists of Geisel Library and the Sally T. WongAvery Library, with off-campus locations at Scripps Archives and Library Annex, the Trade Street Storage Annex, and the UC Southern Regional Library Facility.
“If I Bowdlerize adjectives to describe the proposed new library at San Diego I presume that golldarndest would describe the building that Pereira came up with to satisfy the programmed requirements. […] Obviously architects recently have been ivorried about stodginess of libraries, and have done violence to library needs in some of California’s efforts to overconie this stodginess. Pereira has dramatically done the aesthetic task within the scope of library needs. I wish I could talk with my hands on paper to describe the ‘sculptural, tour de force’ building being considered at San Diego.” Donald C. Davidson

Pereira originally conceived of a mushroom-shaped, steel-framed building, but the projected construction and maintenance costs forced him to switch to a reinforced concrete structure. This change of material presented an opportunity for a more sculptural design, as well as opening up interior spaces that would have been bisected by steel trusses. Prior to construction, a 1/2-scale model of one of the outer columns was built and subjected to various tests. It was envisioned that future additions to the original building would form terraced levels around the tower base descending into the canyon. In keeping with the original master plan, these are "deliberately designed to be subordinated to the strong, geometrical form of the existing library."[16] Within its two subterranean levels are the other library sections as well as study spaces and computer labs. The building has been described by Architecture Daily as occupying "a fascinating nexus between brutalism and futurism". Its tower rises 8 stories to a height of 110 ft (33.5 m). The five upper stories of the tower house collections, individual study space, and group study rooms.
